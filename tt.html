Lecture Summaries

Lecture 1: Literature Review – Teaching Literary Texts

Historical Overview: Literature teaching has evolved from the Grammar-Translation Method (early 20th century) focusing on linguistic accuracy and moral instruction, to the Communicative Language Teaching approach (1970s-80s) viewing literature as a means to enhance communicative competence and cultural awareness.
Theoretical Frameworks: Key models include Language-Based Approach (focus on linguistic features), Reader-Response Approach (active reader role), Stylistic Approach (linguistic + literary analysis), and Critical Literacy Approach (questioning cultural assumptions).
Significance: Literature fosters empathy, creativity, and critical thinking, serving as both mirrors (reflecting learner's experiences) and windows (opening to different worlds).

Lecture 2: Qualities of Pupils' Literature

Definition: Pupils' literature refers to texts chosen for suitability for young readers, shaped by accessibility, imagination, cultural values, and age-appropriate themes.
Historical Tension: Between didactic (instructional, moral lessons) and idyllic (aesthetic/escapist, celebrating childhood) functions.
Key Qualities: Effective pupils' literature should have literary quality (aesthetic richness), linguistic accessibility (challenging but not overwhelming), cultural/emotional relevance, pedagogical adaptability, and inclusivity/representation.
Selection Criteria: Teachers should consider learners' age and proficiency, cultural appropriateness, thematic richness, and potential for classroom activities.

Lecture 3: Comprehensible Input

Definition: Comprehensible Input (CI) is language that learners can understand even if some elements are beyond their current level (i+1). In literature teaching, this means selecting, adapting, or scaffolding texts for comprehension.
Theoretical Foundations: Based on Krashen's Monitor Model (acquisition vs. learning) and Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD) (scaffolding supports comprehension).
Scaffolding Types: Three main types: contextual (historical background), linguistic (simplified summaries, glossaries), and visual (maps, images, videos).
Application Strategies: Pre-reading (introduce themes, vocabulary), During-reading (simplified summaries, guided questions), and Post-reading (creative rewriting, role-play).
Based on Lecture 3: Comprehensible Input

Key Connections Across Lectures

Evolution of Literature Teaching: From grammar-focused approaches to student-centered methods that value personal response and cultural context (Lecture 1).
Text Selection Principles: Balancing linguistic accessibility with literary value is essential for both pupils' literature (Lecture 2) and comprehensible input (Lecture 3).
Student-Centered Approaches: All three lectures emphasize the importance of student engagement, whether through reader-response theory (Lecture 1), selecting relatable texts (Lecture 2), or using scaffolding for comprehension (Lecture 3).
Teacher's Role: Evolving from authority to facilitator who guides interpretation, selects appropriate texts, and provides scaffolding for comprehension.
